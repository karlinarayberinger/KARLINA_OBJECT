OPEN_SOURCE: a classification of intellectual property which specifies that the respective intellectual property is licensed by its original author as being open source (which means the following is assumed to be true):

(a) It is legal for any person to make (and to keep) indefinitely many unmodified (or modified) copies of the respective intellectual property.

(b) It is legal for any person to freely distribute or sell indefinitely many unmodified (or modified) copies of the respective intellectual property.

(c) It is legal for any person to edit indefinitely many unmodified (or modified) copies of the respective intellectual property.

(d) It is legal for any person to claim authorship of indefinitely many unmodified (or modified) copies of the respective intellectual property.

(e) Upon licensing a piece of intellectual property as “open source” and sharing that piece of intellectual property with the public, that piece of intellectual property is treated as being forever after its release “open source” intellectual property.

According to this particular definition of “open source”, logical contradictions can emerge as follows:

STEP_0: Person_A creates an original piece of intellectual property, X. // Initially, and by default, X is privately owned by Person_A. Therefore, only Person_A has "legal authority" over how X is shared with and used by persons other than Person_A (and Person_A is "legally authorized" to prohibit any person other than Person_A from accessing X).

STEP_1: Person_A decides to license X as PUBLIC_DOMAIN intellectual property. // At that point, X can be "legally" copied by any person and those copies can be "legally" treated as the private property of whoever makes or acquires those copies of X.

STEP_2: Person_B decides to make a copy of X and name that copy Y. // Initially, and by default, Y is privately owned by Person_B. Therefore, only Person_B has "legal authority" over how Y is shared with and used by persons other than Person_B (and Person_B is "legally authorized" to prohibit any person other than Person_B from accessing Y).

STEP_3: Person_B decides to license Y as private property and accuses Person_A's X as "illegally" copying Person_B's Y. // "Legally" speaking, Person_A is entitled to own, distribute, and assign an access level (i.e. intellectual property license) to each copy of X Person_A holds even though those copies of X appear to be identical to copies of Y because Person_A was an original creator of X. Also "legally" speaking, Person_B is "legally authorized" to require that people obtain Person_B's permission to make copies of Y. The point of this pseudocode snippet is to illustrate the futility of trying to enforce copyright laws (according to karbytes)! It is hence best (according to karbytes) to license one's own original intellectual property as PUBLIC_DOMAIN to ensure that such intellectual property is maximally accessible to information processing agents in general (which seems to be the ultimate purpose of creating and publishing intellectual property: to maximize the distribution and longevity of one's own created and/or published intellectual property).
